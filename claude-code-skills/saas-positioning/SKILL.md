# SaaS Product Positioning

Build a complete, actionable positioning strategy for a SaaS product using
April Dunford's Obviously Awesome methodology as the backbone, enhanced with
SaaS-specific frameworks for intangible products, multi-stakeholder sales,
and category convergence.

This skill produces a **14-section positioning document** that a sales team
can use tomorrow and a marketing team can execute against this quarter.

---

## When to Use

- Creating positioning for a new SaaS product pre-launch
- Repositioning an existing SaaS product that has stalled
- Entering a new market segment or vertical
- Preparing for a funding round (investors evaluate positioning clarity)
- Competitive landscape has shifted and current positioning feels stale
- Sales cycles are lengthening despite product improvements
- Customers describe the product differently than the company does

## When NOT to Use

- Physical products (not SaaS-specific)
- Internal tools with no external market
- Brand identity / visual design work (that is a separate exercise)
- Tagline or slogan writing in isolation (taglines are outputs of positioning, not inputs)

## Prerequisites — What the User Must Provide

Before generating positioning, gather these inputs. If the user cannot provide
all of them, work with what is available and flag gaps in the output.

**Required:**
- Product name and URL (if live)
- What the product does (1-2 sentences)
- Who currently buys it (or who it is built for if pre-launch)
- Known competitors (even a rough list)

**Strongly recommended:**
- Pricing model and tiers
- Current stage: pre-launch, early traction (<$1M ARR), growth ($1M-$10M ARR), scale ($10M+ ARR), or repositioning
- What customers say they love about the product (verbatim quotes if available)
- What customers were using before switching (competitive alternatives from their perspective)
- Any existing positioning statement, tagline, or elevator pitch

**Optional enrichment:**
- Customer interview transcripts or summaries
- Win/loss analysis data
- NPS or CSAT data
- Sales cycle length and conversion rates
- Churn reasons

If the user provides minimal input, use the Discovery Process (Section 4) to

## Core Principles

These principles govern every decision in the positioning process.

### Positioning Is Context, Not Messaging

Positioning sets the context that makes your value obvious to the right customer.
It is the strategic foundation. Messaging, taglines, ad copy, and website hero
text are all **downstream outputs** of positioning.

If you skip positioning and jump to messaging, you get generic copy that could
describe any competitor. The test: "If I replaced our company name with a
competitor's name, would this positioning still make sense?" If yes, it is not
positioning — it is filler.

Positioning answers: "How should the customer think about us relative to
everything else they could do?" Messaging answers: "What words do we use
to communicate that?"

### SaaS-Specific Positioning Challenges

Six challenges unique to SaaS that must be addressed throughout the process:

**1. Intangibility / Trust Gap**

Software is invisible. Buyers cannot hold it, see it on a shelf, or physically
evaluate it before committing. This creates a trust gap that positioning must
bridge with tangible proof: screenshots, ROI calculators, customer testimonials,
free trials, and "see it in action" anchors. Every claim in the positioning
document needs a proof mechanism.

**2. Constant Product Evolution**

SaaS ships weekly. Features added in Q2 may reshape competitive advantage by Q4.
Positioning must be anchored in **durable value themes** (speed, accuracy,
simplicity, control) rather than feature lists that expire. Features are evidence
for value themes, not the positioning itself.

**3. Multi-Stakeholder Sales**

B2B SaaS sells to committees: the champion (finds and advocates), the budget
holder (approves spend), the technical evaluator (assesses fit), and the end user
(lives with it daily). Each cares about different value:

| Stakeholder | Cares About | Positioning Altitude |
|------------|-------------|---------------------|
| Executive / Budget Holder | Strategic value: ROI, risk reduction, competitive advantage | "What does this mean for the business?" |
| Manager / Champion | Operational value: team productivity, workflow improvement, time savings | "How does this make my team better?" |
| End User | Functional value: ease of use, daily workflow, feature quality | "How does this make my job easier?" |
| Technical Evaluator | Integration, security, reliability, scalability | "Will this work with our stack and not break?" |

Positioning must work at all altitudes. The 14-section document addresses this
with messaging hierarchy (Section 8) that maps messages to each stakeholder.

**4. Feature-Led Thinking Trap**

SaaS teams default to "we have feature X" positioning. Features are inputs.
Value is the output. The "So What?" chain (Component 3) converts features to
value. If your positioning leads with features, you are competing on spec sheets
and will lose to whoever adds the feature next.

**5. Category Convergence**

SaaS categories blur. CRM absorbs email marketing. Project management absorbs
docs. Analytics absorbs BI. Positioning must either defend a clear category
boundary or deliberately choose to cross it. Ignoring category convergence leads
to "we do everything" positioning, which means "we do nothing well."

**6. Switching Cost Asymmetry**

Switching SaaS feels easy (it is cloud software, just cancel) but is actually
hard (data migration, team retraining, integration rewiring, workflow disruption).
Positioning can leverage this: for incumbents, emphasize the hidden cost of
switching away. For challengers, emphasize ease of adoption and migration support.

### Three Positioning Styles

Every SaaS product must choose one of three positioning styles. This choice
determines category strategy, competitive framing, and go-to-market approach.

| Style | When to Use | Risk | SaaS Example |
|-------|------------|------|-------------|
| **Head-to-Head** | Category is established, you have a credible claim to leadership or clear advantage on existing buying criteria | Must actually win on the criteria buyers already use | Notion vs. Confluence, Linear vs. Jira |
| **Big Fish, Small Pond** (Subcategory) | Cannot win the whole market but can dominate a specific segment | Market may be too small; hard to expand later | Vanta (compliance for startups), Gusto (payroll for SMBs) |
| **Create New Category** | Product does not fit any existing category; enables something fundamentally new | "Education tax" — must spend heavily to teach the market the category exists | Slack (2013 — team messaging), Figma (collaborative design), Gong (revenue intelligence) |

**Decision tree:**

```
Can you win the established category on existing buying criteria?
├── YES → Head-to-Head
└── NO
    ├── Can you find a segment where you ARE the obvious choice?
    │   ├── YES → Big Fish, Small Pond
    │   └── NO
    │       ├── Does your product enable something fundamentally new?
    │       │   ├── YES → Create New Category
    │       │   └── NO → You have a product problem, not a positioning problem.
    │       │           Fix the product before fixing the positioning.
    └── UNSURE → Default to Big Fish, Small Pond. It is the safest strategy
                  for most startups. You can expand the pond later.
```

**Important:** Most SaaS startups should default to **Big Fish, Small Pond**.
Creating a new category requires $5M+ in marketing spend and 12-18 months of
category evangelism before the market even understands what you are selling.
Head-to-Head requires being genuinely better on the criteria buyers already
use — not just different.

## Discovery Process

If the user cannot provide sufficient prerequisites, guide them through
discovery before generating the positioning document.

### Step 0: Gather Raw Material

Use the questions in `references/discovery-questions.md` to interview the user
about their product, customers, competitors, and market. Adapt questions based
on the user's stage (pre-launch vs. growth vs. repositioning).

### Jobs-To-Be-Done (JTBD) Discovery

Use JTBD when customer data is thin — when the user cannot articulate why
customers buy or what they were using before.

**The Four Forces of Progress:**

Every purchase decision has four forces acting on it:

```
PUSH (away from current solution)         PULL (toward new solution)
├── Frustration with status quo           ├── Attraction of new approach
├── "This isn't working anymore"          ├── "This could solve my problem"
└── Trigger event (broke, outgrew,        └── Social proof ("others use this")
    regulation changed)

ANXIETY (about switching)                 HABIT (staying with current)
├── "What if the new thing is worse?"     ├── "I already know how to use this"
├── "Migration will be painful"           ├── "Good enough"
└── "My team will resist change"          └── "Switching takes time I don't have"
```

For positioning to work, PUSH + PULL must exceed ANXIETY + HABIT.
Positioning can amplify Push and Pull while reducing Anxiety.

**JTBD Interview Questions:**

Ask these about recent customers (purchased in last 90 days):

1. "What were you using before us? Walk me through a typical day with that solution."
2. "What was the moment you decided the old way wasn't working anymore?" (Push)
3. "What first made you aware of us / products like ours?" (Pull)
4. "What almost stopped you from switching? What concerns did you have?" (Anxiety)
5. "When you describe us to a colleague, what do you say?"
6. "What can you do now that you couldn't do before?"
7. "If we disappeared tomorrow, what would you switch to?"

**Clustering:** Group the answers from 5-8 interviews into themes. The themes
that appear 3+ times across interviews are your positioning inputs:
- Answers to Q1 and Q7 → Competitive Alternatives (Component 1)
- Answers to Q2 → Push forces and trigger events → Target Customer triggers
- Answers to Q5 → How customers naturally position you (use their language)
- Answers to Q6 → Value themes (Component 3)

### The "What Would You Switch To?" Test

This single question is the most powerful competitive intelligence tool in
positioning. Ask existing customers: "If our product disappeared tomorrow,
what would you use instead?"

Their answers ARE your competitive alternatives — not what you think they are.
The most common answer is your primary competitive alternative. If customers
say "Excel spreadsheets" instead of naming a direct competitor, your real
competition is spreadsheets, and your positioning must beat spreadsheets,
not the SaaS tool you think you compete with.

## The 6-Component Positioning Framework

This is the core of the skill. Work through all 6 components **in order**.
Each component builds on the previous one. Do not skip ahead.

### Component 1: Competitive Alternatives

**Question:** What would your best customers use if your product did not exist?

This is NOT a list of companies you consider competitors. This is a list of
what your customers would actually do instead. There are 5 types:

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Direct competitors** | Same category, same job | Slack vs. Teams |
| **Adjacent products** | Different category, same job | Notion vs. Google Docs + Trello + Confluence |
| **Manual/DIY workarounds** | Spreadsheets, email, pen and paper | "We tracked everything in a shared Google Sheet" |
| **Hiring someone** | Agency, contractor, or new employee | "We'd hire a junior analyst to do this manually" |
| **Do nothing** | Status quo, live with the pain | "We'd just keep doing it the old way" |

**Process:**
1. List every alternative your customers have mentioned (from interviews, sales calls, churn surveys)
2. For each, note how often customers mention it (frequency matters)
3. Rank by frequency — the top 3-5 are your real competitive alternatives
4. For each top alternative, document: what it does well, where it falls short, and why customers leave it

**Output format — Competitive Alternatives Table:**

| Alternative | Type | Frequency | What It Does Well | Where It Falls Short | Why Customers Leave |
|------------|------|-----------|------------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| [Name] | Direct / Adjacent / Manual / Hire / Status Quo | High / Medium / Low | | | |

**SaaS-specific note:** In SaaS, "do nothing" and "manual workaround" are often
the #1 and #2 competitive alternatives, especially for new categories. Do not
dismiss them. If your biggest competitor is a spreadsheet, your positioning must
make the case for why a purpose-built tool is worth paying for.

### Component 2: Unique Attributes

**Question:** What do you have that the competitive alternatives do NOT?

Start with a brain dump of every feature, capability, and characteristic of your
product. Then apply two filters:

**Filter 1 — The Uniqueness Test:**
For each attribute, ask: "Do any of our competitive alternatives also have this?"
- If YES → Remove it. It is not unique.
- If PARTIALLY → Keep it, but note it is a "degree" advantage, not a "kind" advantage.
- If NO → This is a unique attribute. Keep it.

**Filter 2 — The Truth Test:**
For each remaining attribute, ask: "Can we prove this with evidence?"
- If YES → Verified unique attribute.
- If NO → It is an aspiration, not an attribute. Remove it or flag it as "aspirational."

**What counts as a unique attribute:**
- Features (only if no competitor has them)
- Technology approach (e.g., AI-native architecture vs. AI bolted on)
- Business model (e.g., usage-based pricing when everyone else is per-seat)
- Data advantage (e.g., proprietary dataset, network effects)
- Focus (e.g., built exclusively for one vertical when competitors serve many)
- Experience/expertise (e.g., founded by domain experts)
- Integration depth (e.g., native integration with specific platform)
- Speed of delivery / time-to-value
- Customer experience quality (support, onboarding, community)

**Clustering:**
Group related unique attributes into 3-5 clusters. Each cluster should
represent a coherent theme. More than 7 clusters means you are not grouping
tightly enough. Fewer than 3 means you may not have enough differentiation
(which is a product problem, not a positioning problem).

**Output format — Unique Attributes by Cluster:**

| Cluster Name | Unique Attributes | Uniqueness Level | Evidence |
|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|----------|
| [Theme] | Attr 1, Attr 2, Attr 3 | Only us / Degree advantage | [How we prove it] |

### Component 3: Value Mapping (The "So What?" Chain)

**Question:** What value do these unique attributes deliver to the customer?

Features are not value. Value is what the customer gets as a result of the
features. The "So What?" chain bridges the gap:

```
Feature → Capability → Benefit → Business Outcome
```

For each unique attribute cluster from Component 2, walk the chain:

**Example:**
```
Feature:          "AI-powered anomaly detection"
  → So what?
Capability:       "Catches errors that manual review misses"
  → So what?
Benefit:          "Fewer production incidents slip through"
  → So what?
Business Outcome: "99.95% uptime SLA compliance — no revenue-impacting downtime"
```

The business outcome at the end of the chain is your **value theme**.

**Process:**
1. Take each unique attribute cluster
2. Walk the "So What?" chain until you reach a business outcome
3. Group the business outcomes into 2-4 **value themes**
4. For each value theme, identify at least one **proof point**

**SaaS Value Categories:**
Map each value theme to one of these categories:

| Category | Description | Example |
|----------|------------|---------|
| **Revenue impact** | Increase revenue, reduce churn, expand accounts | "Customers using our tool see 23% lower churn" |
| **Cost reduction** | Eliminate tools, reduce headcount needs, lower infrastructure costs | "Replaces 3 separate tools at half the total cost" |
| **Time savings** | Hours saved per week/month, faster processes | "Reduces report generation from 4 hours to 15 minutes" |
| **Risk reduction** | Compliance, security, reliability, audit readiness | "Automates SOC 2 compliance — audit-ready in weeks, not months" |
| **Strategic advantage** | Speed to market, competitive intelligence, innovation capacity | "Ship features 3x faster with automated testing pipeline" |

**Proof points** — each value theme needs at least one:
- Customer quote (verbatim, attributed if possible)
- Metric (before/after comparison)
- Case study reference (company name, result achieved)
- Third-party validation (analyst report, award, certification)

**Output format — Value Map:**

| Value Theme | "So What?" Chain | SaaS Value Category | Proof Point | Stakeholder |
|------------|-----------------|--------------------|-----------|--------------------|
| [2-4 words] | Feature → ... → Outcome | Revenue / Cost / Time / Risk / Strategic | [Evidence] | Exec / Manager / User / Technical |

### Component 4: Best-Fit Target Customers

**Question:** Who gets the MOST value from your unique attributes?

This is NOT "who could use this product." Every SaaS product could theoretically
serve thousands of companies. Best-fit customers are the ones who:
- Have the problem your unique attributes solve most acutely
- Get disproportionate value from your specific approach
- Close faster, churn less, and expand more than other segments
- Become advocates and refer others

**Actionable Segmentation Criteria:**

Segments must be identifiable from the outside (you can build a target list
without talking to the company first).

| Criterion | Examples | How to Identify |
|-----------|---------|----------------|
| **Company size** | 50-200 employees, $5M-$50M revenue | LinkedIn, Crunchbase, company website |
| **Industry/vertical** | Fintech, healthcare SaaS, e-commerce | Company description, industry tags |
| **Tech stack** | Uses Salesforce, runs on AWS, has Slack | Job postings, BuiltWith, Stackshare |
| **Organizational trigger** | Just raised Series A, hired VP of X, hit 50 employees | News alerts, LinkedIn, Crunchbase |
| **Pain signal** | Regulatory change, competitor pressure, growth bottleneck | Industry news, job postings (hiring for roles your tool replaces) |
| **Geography** | US-based, EU (GDPR-affected), APAC | Company HQ location |

**Define both ICP and Buyer Persona:**

**ICP (Ideal Customer Profile)** = company-level definition
- Company size range
- Industry/vertical
- Tech stack requirements
- Organizational maturity signals
- Revenue range

**Buyer Persona** = person-level definition within the ICP
- Job title / role
- Seniority level
- Daily pain points
- Buying authority (decision maker, influencer, or user)
- How they evaluate solutions (demo-first, trial-first, peer-referral)

**Anti-Persona** = who should NOT buy (and why)
Define this explicitly. It prevents bad-fit customers from entering the funnel,
which reduces churn, support burden, and negative reviews. Examples:
- "Companies with fewer than 20 employees — our tool's value scales with team size"
- "Teams without a dedicated ops person — setup requires technical configuration"

**Validation Tests:**

| Test | Question | Pass Criteria |
|------|----------|--------------|
| **The List Test** | Can you generate a list of 100+ companies matching this segment using LinkedIn, Crunchbase, or similar? | If yes, segment is actionable. If no, it is too vague. |
| **The Sales Test** | When your sales team calls this segment, do they get meetings? | If yes, positioning resonates. If no, message or segment is wrong. |
| **The Ad Test** | Can you target this segment with paid ads (LinkedIn, Google, Meta)? | If yes, segment is reachable. If no, it is not specific enough for marketing. |

### Component 5: Market Category

**Question:** What is the mental "bucket" customers put you in?

Market category is the context that tells customers what to expect. When you say
"CRM," buyers immediately know: it manages customer relationships, it has a
pipeline view, it integrates with email. Category does the heavy lifting of
setting expectations so your positioning can focus on differentiation.

**Three Category Strategies:**

**Strategy 1: Use an Existing Category**
- Ride the existing understanding — buyers know what the category is
- Best when the category is growing and you can credibly claim leadership or a clear advantage
- Positioning focuses on WHY you are better within the category
- Risk: you inherit the category's baggage (expectations, pricing norms, feature checklists)

**Strategy 2: Subcategory (Modifier + Known Category)**
- Take an existing category and add a modifier that narrows focus
- Pattern: "[Modifier] + [Known Category]"
- Examples: "CRM for real estate," "AI-powered customer support," "Developer-first analytics"
- Best when you cannot win the whole category but dominate a niche
- Positioning focuses on why the modifier matters (why this segment needs a specialized solution)
- Risk: the niche may be too small for venture-scale growth

**Strategy 3: Create a New Category**
- Define a new space that does not exist yet
- Required when your product genuinely does not fit any existing category
- Most expensive approach — you must spend to educate the market
- **Education Tax:** The cost of teaching buyers what your category is. Estimate
  6-18 months of content marketing, founder evangelism, analyst briefings, and
  paid media before the market understands. Budget $500K-$5M+ depending on
  market size.
- Positioning focuses on WHY the new category needs to exist and what is broken
  about the old categories
- Risk: market may not adopt the category, or a larger player may co-opt it

**Category Test:** Say your category out loud to someone outside your company.
- If they immediately understand what kind of product you are → Category works.
- If they say "what does that mean?" → Category problem.
- If they say "oh, so it's like [wrong comparison]?" → Category is misleading.

**SaaS Category Naming Patterns:**

| Pattern | Example | When It Works |
|---------|---------|--------------|
| [Verb] + [Noun] | "Revenue Intelligence" (Gong) | New category creation |
| [Audience] + [Category] | "Developer Analytics" | Subcategory, audience-first |
| [Modifier] + [Category] | "AI-Powered CRM" | Subcategory, technology-first |
| [Outcome] + Platform | "Customer Success Platform" | Outcome-focused subcategory |
| [Industry] + [Category] | "Healthcare CRM" | Vertical SaaS subcategory |

**Pricing-as-Positioning:**
Your pricing model communicates positioning. Per-seat pricing signals
collaboration tool. Usage-based pricing signals infrastructure/utility.
Flat-rate pricing signals simplicity. Freemium signals product-led growth.
Ensure pricing model reinforces the category and positioning — do not let
pricing contradict the story.

**Multi-Product Consideration:**
If the company has multiple products or product lines, decide whether to:
- **Unified platform positioning** — one positioning for the whole platform
  (best when products are tightly integrated and sold together)
- **Product-level positioning** — separate positioning for each product
  (best when products serve different buyers or different jobs)
- **Branded House** — one brand, one positioning, products are features
  (Salesforce: Sales Cloud, Service Cloud, Marketing Cloud — all "Salesforce")

Most SaaS startups should use unified platform positioning until they have
3+ distinct products serving meaningfully different buyers.

### Component 6: Relevant Trends

**Question:** What macro trends make your positioning more credible right now?

Trends add urgency and inevitability to your positioning. They answer "why now?"
— why this product, in this category, for these customers, at this moment.

**Connection Requirement:** The trend must connect to your **unique value**,
not just your market. Every SaaS company can claim "AI is hot." Only your
positioning should explain why the trend makes YOUR specific value more relevant.

**Good trend connection:**
"The shift to remote-first teams means real-time async collaboration is
essential — and our product is the only one that enables async video review
with inline commenting, replacing the 6-meeting review cycle."

**Bad trend connection:**
"AI is transforming every industry and we use AI too."
(No connection to unique value. Every competitor can say this.)

**SaaS-Relevant Trend Categories:**

| Trend Category | Examples | Connection Test |
|---------------|---------|----------------|
| AI / Automation | AI-native workflows, copilots, autonomous agents | "Our AI does X that generic AI cannot because of Y" |
| Remote / Hybrid Work | Async collaboration, distributed teams, digital-first ops | "Remote teams need X, which only we provide" |
| Consolidation | Fewer tools, platform plays, all-in-one suites | "We replace N tools with one platform" |
| Privacy / Compliance | GDPR, SOC 2, data residency, AI governance | "Our approach to X ensures compliance with Y" |
| Vertical SaaS | Industry-specific solutions replacing horizontal tools | "Generic tools fail at X industry requirement" |
| Product-Led Growth | Self-serve, bottoms-up adoption, free tiers | "Users adopt without needing sales involvement" |
| Usage-Based Pricing | Pay for what you use, consumption models | "Our model aligns cost with value delivered" |

**Output:** 1-3 trends, each with a clear connection to a specific unique
attribute or value theme. Do not list trends without connecting them.

## Output: The Positioning Document

When this skill is invoked, produce ALL 14 sections below using the exact
headings. Each section includes format instructions and quality criteria.

### Section 1: Executive Summary

**Length:** 200-300 words
**Structure:** Who we are (category + target) → What makes us different (top 2-3
value themes) → Why now (trend) → The positioning statement

This section must be **standalone** — a reader who reads only this section should
understand the positioning. It is the "elevator pitch on paper."

**Quality test:** Could a new sales hire read this and explain the product to a
prospect without any other context? If not, rewrite.

### Section 2: Market Category & Frame of Reference

**Content:**
- Chosen category and the strategy (Head-to-Head / Subcategory / New Category)
- Rationale for the strategy choice (why this one, why not the alternatives)
- Adjacent categories considered and why they were rejected
- Category maturity assessment (emerging / growing / mature / declining)
- Education tax estimate if creating a new category or subcategory
- How pricing model reinforces the category

**Quality test:** Say the category to someone unfamiliar with the product. Do they
immediately understand what kind of product it is?

### Section 3: Competitive Landscape Analysis

**Format:** Table + narrative analysis

**Table:**

| Competitor | Category | Target Segment | Key Strength | Key Weakness | Pricing Model | How They Position Themselves |
|-----------|----------|---------------|-------------|-------------|--------------|----------------------------|
| [Name] | | | | | | |

Include at minimum:
- 3-5 direct competitors
- 1-2 adjacent alternatives
- The primary manual/DIY workaround

**Narrative analysis (after the table):**
- Market dynamics: is the market consolidating, fragmenting, or stable?
- Competitive gaps: where is the white space?
- Threat assessment: which competitor is most dangerous and why?
- Differentiation durability: how long before competitors copy your advantages?

**Quality test:** Would the sales team use this table in competitive deals?

### Section 4: Unique Attributes Matrix

**Format:** Comparison matrix

| Attribute | Our Product | Competitor A | Competitor B | Competitor C | Manual/DIY |
|-----------|------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| [Attr 1] | Yes | No | Partial | No | No |
| [Attr 2] | Yes | No | No | No | No |
| [Attr 3] | Yes | Yes | Yes | No | No |

- Mark each cell: **Yes** (has it), **Partial** (has it partially), **No** (lacks it)
- Rows where only "Our Product" has "Yes" are the positioning foundation
- Include 8-15 attributes for a meaningful comparison
- Group attributes by cluster from Component 2

**Quality test:** Are the "Yes" claims provable? Can a prospect verify them?

### Section 5: Value Themes & Proof Points

**Format:** For each value theme (2-4 themes):

```
## Value Theme: [2-4 word name]

**"So What?" Chain:**
[Feature] → [Capability] → [Benefit] → [Business Outcome]

**SaaS Value Category:** Revenue / Cost / Time / Risk / Strategic

**Primary Stakeholder:** Executive / Manager / User / Technical

**Proof Points:**
- [Customer quote, metric, case study, or third-party validation]
- [Second proof point if available]

**One-liner for sales:** "[Concise statement of this value theme with proof]"
```

**Quality test:** For each value theme, can you point to at least one real
customer or real metric that proves it?

### Section 6: Best-Fit Customer Definition

**Format:**


**Quality test:** Run the List Test — can you generate 100 companies matching

### Section 7: Positioning Statement

**Primary format (For/Who/Is/That/Unlike/We):**

> **For** [target customer segment with identifying characteristics]
> **Who** [key need, pain point, or job-to-be-done]
> **[Product name] is a** [market category]
> **That** [primary value proposition — the #1 reason to choose us]
> **Unlike** [primary competitive alternative — what they would use instead]
> **We** [primary differentiator — what we do that the alternative cannot]

**Short-form version** (1 sentence, under 25 words, for website hero and pitch decks):

> [Product name] is the [category modifier] that [primary value] for [target customer].

**Alternative formats** (provide both if useful):

**Sequoia format:** One declarative sentence defining the company.
> "[Product] does X for Y by Z."

**Category-first format:** Leads with the market context.
> "In a world where [trend/problem], [Product] is the first [category] that [differentiator]."

**Quality test:** Read the positioning statement out loud. Does it sound like
something a human would say to explain the product at a dinner party? If it
sounds like corporate jargon, rewrite in plain language.

```
## Messaging by Altitude
```

**PLG vs. Sales-Led Note:**
- **Product-led growth (PLG):** Messaging must work without a salesperson. The
  website, onboarding flow, and in-product copy must communicate positioning
  on their own. Prioritize the User Pitch and make the Executive Pitch visible
  on the pricing page.
- **Sales-led:** Messaging is delivered by humans. Provide talk tracks, not just
  written copy. The Executive Pitch and Manager Pitch are most critical.

**Quality test:** Give the messaging hierarchy to 3 different team members
(sales, marketing, product). Can each one use it immediately without asking
for clarification?

### Section 9: Sales Narrative (8-Step)

Use the template in `references/sales-narrative-template.md`. Produce a
complete narrative with talk tracks for each step.

**The 8 Steps:**

1. **Setup (The World Has Changed)** — Define the relevant market trend or
   insight that sets the context. This is NOT about your product. It is about
   the customer's world.

2. **Problem (What This Creates)** — Articulate the specific problem this
   trend creates for your target customer. Use their language.

3. **Current Approaches** — How customers try to solve it today. List the
   competitive alternatives and what each does.

4. **Gaps** — Where those approaches fall short. Be specific: "Spreadsheets
   work until you have 50+ team members, then they break because..."

5. **The Perfect World** — What the ideal solution would look like. Paint the
   picture before introducing your product.

6. **Introduction** — Your product, your category, one sentence. "That is why
   we built [Product], the [category] that [primary value]."

7. **Value Proof** — Walk through each value theme with evidence. This is where
   demos, metrics, and case studies live. Map to stakeholder altitudes.

8. **Next Step** — Specific action: free trial, demo, POC, pilot. Make it
   concrete and low-risk.

**For each step, include:**
- Talk track (what to say, in natural language)
- Key phrases to use
- Phrases to avoid
- If applicable: top objection at this step and how to handle it
- Transition to next step

**Quality test:** Could a new sales rep deliver this narrative in a prospect
meeting after one read-through?

### Section 13: Validation Plan

Positioning is a hypothesis until validated. Define specific tests:

**The Three Core Tests:**

| Test | Method | Timeline | Pass Criteria |
|------|--------|----------|--------------|
| **The List Test** | Generate target account list of 100+ companies matching the ICP using LinkedIn/Crunchbase | Week 1 | Can generate 100+ matches. If not, ICP is too narrow or too vague. |

### Section 14: When to Revisit

Positioning is not permanent. Plan to revisit when any of these triggers occur:

1. **Entering a new segment or vertical** — The positioning that works for your
   current ICP may not resonate with the new audience.

2. **Launching a major new product or feature** — If a new capability
   fundamentally changes your competitive advantage, positioning must reflect it.

3. **A well-funded competitor enters your category** — New entrants change the
   competitive landscape and may force a category or differentiation shift.

4. **Growth has stalled for 2+ quarters despite execution** — If the team is
   executing well but growth is flat, positioning may be the bottleneck.

5. **M&A activity** — Acquiring a company or being acquired changes the story.

6. **Customer language has shifted** — If customer interviews reveal they
   describe you differently than 6-12 months ago, your positioning may be
   outdated even if it was once accurate.

**When NOT to revisit:**
- Because you are bored with the messaging (positioning needs 6-12 months to work)
- Because a competitor changed their positioning (react only if it impacts your differentiation)
- Because a new marketing hire wants to "put their stamp on it"

## Reference Material

### Common Positioning Mistakes

1. **Positioning for everyone.** "We help companies grow" positions you for
   nobody. The narrower the target, the sharper the positioning. You can
   expand the target later — you cannot un-dilute generic positioning.

2. **Confusing positioning with tagline.** The tagline is a compressed
   expression of positioning. It comes AFTER the positioning work, not before.
   Starting with tagline brainstorming produces empty slogans.

3. **Anchoring on features instead of value.** "We have 200+ integrations"
   is a feature. "Connect your entire stack in minutes, not months" is value.
   Always run the "So What?" chain.

4. **Choosing a category nobody understands.** If you have to explain what
   the category means, you are paying the education tax. Most startups cannot
   afford it. Use a modifier + known category instead.

5. **Ignoring what customers say their alternatives are.** Your competition
   is what CUSTOMERS say it is, not what you think. If they say "we were using
   spreadsheets," your competitor is spreadsheets.

6. **Positioning by committee.** Too many stakeholders produce mediocre
   consensus. One person (usually the CEO or head of product marketing) must
   own the final positioning. Others contribute inputs, one person decides.

7. **"We're like X but better."** This is not positioning. It is a comparison.
   And it positions X as the standard and you as the derivative. Define your
   own frame of reference.

8. **Changing positioning every quarter.** Positioning needs 6-12 months to
   permeate every touchpoint and register with the market. Changing it too
   frequently means it never has time to work.

### Red Flags That Positioning Is Broken

If any of these are true, positioning needs work:

- Sales cycles are getting longer despite product improvements
- Win rate is declining or flat in competitive deals
- Customers describe your product differently than you do
- You compete on price because buyers see no differentiation
- Website bounce rate is high on landing pages
- "What do you do?" is hard to answer at a dinner party
- New hires take weeks to articulate what the company does
- Marketing and sales describe the product differently
- Product roadmap is driven by competitor features, not customer value
- Every deal requires heavy discounting to close

## Quality Rules

These rules apply to EVERY section of the positioning document output.
Enforce them ruthlessly.

**1. No fluff, no filler.**
Every sentence must add information or insight. Delete anything that sounds like
it came from a consulting template. Test: read each paragraph and ask "what
would be lost if I deleted this?" If nothing, delete it.

**2. Evidence-backed claims only.**
Every assertion about the market, customers, or competitors must cite a source:
customer quote, market data, product evidence, or third-party reference.
"We believe..." is not evidence. "Based on interviews with 8 customers..." is.
If you do not have evidence, say "hypothesis — needs validation" explicitly.

**3. Specific, not generic.**
"We help companies grow" fails. "We reduce SaaS onboarding time from 14 days
to 2 days for Series A fintech companies" works. Every claim should fail the
"could a competitor say this too?" test. If yes, it is not specific enough.

**4. Customer language, not jargon.**
Use the words customers use in interviews and reviews, not internal product
terminology or marketing buzzwords. If customers say "we needed to stop losing
deals," do not write "revenue optimization challenges." Mirror their language.

**5. Actionable recommendations.**
Every recommendation in the 30/60/90 plan must have: a specific action, an
owner (role, not person name), a deadline (relative), and a success metric.
"Improve messaging" fails. "Rewrite homepage hero to lead with [value theme],
measured by homepage-to-signup conversion rate, by Day 15" works.

**6. The Sales Team Test.**
After generating the document, mentally ask: "Would the sales team use this
tomorrow in a prospect call?" If any section feels theoretical or abstract,
rewrite it with concrete talk tracks, objection responses, and proof points.

**7. Honest assessment.**
If the product has weak differentiation, say so. If the target market is too
broad, flag it. If the competitive landscape is brutal, acknowledge it. The
positioning document must be a truthful strategic analysis, not a marketing
brochure. Honesty builds trust and produces better strategy.

## Process Summary

When this skill is invoked, follow this sequence:

1. **Gather inputs** — Collect prerequisites from user. If incomplete, use Discovery Process.
2. **Triage** — Run Quick Diagnostic if user has existing positioning. Determine mode.
3. **Work the 6 Components** — In order: Alternatives → Attributes → Value → Customers → Category → Trends
4. **Generate the 14-section document** — Using the Output specification above.
5. **Apply Quality Rules** — Review every section against the 7 rules.
6. **Deliver** — Present the complete document. Highlight the top 3 actions for immediate execution.
