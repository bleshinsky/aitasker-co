# Anti-Patterns — Hard Blocks

These are hallmarks of generic AI-generated or template-based design. Do not produce any of them.

## Design Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | What to Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| Inter, Roboto, Arial, or system fonts as primary typography | Generic, forgettable, zero personality. These are the fonts that say "I didn't think about typography." | Choose distinctive fonts from Google Fonts. Pair a characterful display font with a refined body font. |
| Purple gradient on white background | The #1 AI-slop cliché. Instantly signals "generated by AI" to design-literate visitors. | Choose a palette with personality tied to the brand context. If using gradients, make them subtle and between related hues. |
| Uniform rounded corners on everything | Looks like a UI kit demo, not a designed experience. Signals template rather than intention. | Vary border-radius intentionally. Mix sharp edges with selective rounding. Let the aesthetic direction dictate the shape language. |
| Excessive centered layouts with symmetric cards | Predictable, boring, identical to every other template site. No visual tension or flow. | Use asymmetric grids, off-center compositions, alternating full-bleed and constrained sections. Create visual rhythm. |
| Space Grotesk as the default heading font | Common AI convergence — many AI-generated sites land on this font. | Rotate through distinctive options. No single font should become your default across designs. |
| Flat solid-color backgrounds with no texture or depth | Lifeless, sterile, no atmosphere. Makes the page feel like a wireframe with content. | Add subtle gradients, noise/grain, geometric patterns, layered transparencies, or mesh gradients. Create depth. |
| Animations on everything with no choreography | Distracting, overwhelming, reduces clarity. Motion should guide, not scatter. | Focus on one orchestrated moment (page load stagger) + purposeful scroll reveals. Less is more when each animation is intentional. |
| Generic stock illustration style (isometric people, abstract blobs) | Signals "we didn't invest in this" to visitors. Feels corporate-generic. | Use real screenshots, custom graphics, photography, or at minimum, illustrations with a distinctive style tied to the brand. |

## CRO Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | What to Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| Vague hero headlines ("Welcome to the future," "Innovate today") | Zero information content, zero conversion impact. Doesn't answer "what is this?" or "why should I care?" | Write specific, benefit-driven headlines: "Cut reporting time from 4 hours to 15 minutes" or "The CRM that closes deals while you sleep." |
| CTAs that say "Submit," "Learn More," or "Click Here" | Weak, passive, low-conversion. "Submit" is what you do to a government form. "Learn More" defers the decision rather than encouraging it. | Use value-communicating copy: "Start free trial," "See pricing," "Get my report," "Book a 15-min demo." The CTA should tell the visitor what they GET. |
| Feature-focused copy instead of benefit-focused | Doesn't answer "why should I care?" Features describe what the product does; benefits describe what the user gains. | Lead with the outcome: "Save 10 hours per week" not "Automated workflow engine." Connect every feature to a human benefit. |
| No trust signals near CTAs | Asking for conversion without building trust. The visitor has no reason to believe your claims when you're asking for their email/money. | Place logos, testimonial snippets, or stats directly adjacent to (above or below) every primary CTA on the page. |
| Too many form fields | Every extra field reduces conversion. Asking for phone number, company size, and job title upfront creates friction that kills signups. | Ask for the absolute minimum to get the visitor started. You can collect more information later. Email + name is often enough. |
| No objection handling before the final CTA | The visitor scrolls to the bottom with unanswered concerns and bounces. | Add an FAQ section or objection-handling block above the final CTA. Address price, fit, difficulty, and risk. |
| Hiding CTAs on mobile behind menus | Mobile visitors should have an always-visible conversion path. Burying the CTA in a hamburger menu kills mobile conversion. | Use sticky bottom CTA bars, inline CTAs, or persistent floating buttons on mobile. |
| Slow page load (> 3 seconds) | Every additional second costs ~7% in conversions. Visitors won't wait for your hero image to load. | Lazy-load below-fold images, minimize render-blocking CSS/JS, use skeleton screens, compress assets aggressively. |
